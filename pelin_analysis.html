Question 3: Did the COVID-19 pandemic lead to a measurable shift in ‘Conservation’ values (Security, Conformity, Tradition) and ‘Political Trust’ between Round 9 and Round 11?

This analysis investigates whether the COVID-19 pandemic led to measurable changes in Conservation values (Security, Conformity, Tradition) and Political Trust in Europe. Using data from the European Social Survey (ESS), we examine three time points: Round 9 (2018) as the pre-pandemic baseline, Round 10 (2020/22) as the pandemic period, and Round 11 (2024) as the post-pandemic landscape.

Note on Data: The dataset uses MRAT-corrected (mean-centered) Schwartz value scores, which remove individual response style bias and allow reliable comparisons across rounds and generations. All three ESS rounds contain complete value data in this version of the dataset.

Method

Two indices are constructed from the MRAT-corrected (mean-centered) Schwartz value scores:

  • Conservation Index: Captures the degree to which individuals prioritize stability, order, and tradition. Calculated as the mean of Security_centered, Conformity_centered, and Tradition_centered. Higher values indicate a stronger preference for conservation-oriented values.
  • Political Trust Index: Measures confidence in political institutions. Operationalized via trstprl (trust in parliament) on a 0–10 scale.

Statistical significance is assessed using independent samples t-tests (α = 0.05), comparing R9 vs R10 and R9 vs R11 for both indices. Generational cohorts are defined as: Gen Z (born ≥1997), Millennials (1981–1996), Gen X (1965–1980), Boomers (1946–1964), and Silent Gen (born ≤1945).

The summary statistics and t-tests provide the following insights:

Conservation Values

R9 vs R10 (Pandemic Period): Conservation scores declined from a pre-pandemic mean of 0.114 (R9) to 0.077 (R10) during the pandemic. This difference is statistically significant (p < 0.001).

R9 vs R11 (Long-Term): By 2024, Conservation values partially recovered to a mean of 0.098, but remained below the pre-pandemic baseline. This difference is also statistically significant (p < 0.001).

The direction of change is counterintuitive: rather than driving Europeans toward greater security-seeking and conformity, the pandemic appears to have slightly loosened conservation-oriented value priorities.

Political Trust

R9 vs R10 (Pandemic Period): Trust in parliament dropped from a pre-pandemic mean of 4.99 (R9) to 4.70 (R10). This decline is statistically significant (p < 0.001).

R9 vs R11 (Long-Term): By 2024, trust measured 4.65, remaining below both the pre-pandemic and pandemic-period levels. This difference is statistically significant (p < 0.001).

The pattern suggests that the pandemic functioned as a lasting trust-eroding event rather than a temporary disruption.

Conclusion

This analysis examined whether the COVID-19 pandemic produced measurable and lasting shifts in Conservation values and Political Trust across European generations, using ESS data spanning 2018 to 2024.

Conservation Values

The results support the intuitive assumption that older generations prioritize conservation values more highly than younger generations. At the aggregate level (all generations combined), Conservation values declined modestly but significantly during the pandemic and, while partially recovering by 2024, did not return to pre-pandemic levels.

Examining generational differences reveals a clear and stable hierarchy across all three rounds (see Figure 1). Silent Gen consistently scores highest on Conservation, followed by Boomers, Gen X, Millennials, and Gen Z scores lowest. This ordering remains unchanged from 2018 to 2024.

The modest aggregate decline in Conservation values, combined with this stable generational hierarchy, suggests that while the pandemic may have slightly loosened conservation-oriented priorities overall, deep-seated generational value differences; with older generations placing greater emphasis on security, conformity, and tradition; remained largely resilient to the shock.

Political Trust

Political trust shows a clear and significant decline.

Figure 2 reveals that generational trajectories differ substantially. Gen Z starts highest (7.5), declines most sharply (to 5.8), and recovers only marginally by R11 (5.9). Silent Gen and Millennials begin at similar levels (≈5.4), but while Millennials drop immediately and partially recover, Silent Gen increases slightly in R10 before declining to 5.0. Boomers increase temporarily in R10 (4.3 → 4.4) before falling to 4.1, whereas Gen X declines steadily to become the lowest-trust group by R11 (4.0). Trust levels do not converge by 2024.

The pandemic eroded political trust across all age groups, but younger generations, who entered the period with higher trust, experienced the largest absolute declines. By 2024, trust levels across generations had largely converged downward.
